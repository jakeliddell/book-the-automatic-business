# THE AUTOMATIC BUSINESS

## Master Project Brief: Who We Are Writing For

This is the north-star reference for the book. It draws together every prior piece of audience work from the "Productise Yourself" project so that every session stays aligned on one reader, one problem, and one voice. Read this first in any new session.

Sources synthesised: ICP Summary, ICP Problem Landscape (surface, hidden, downstream, aspirational), MoSCoW prioritisation, RICE scoring, and the two voice-of-customer research documents ("How Overwhelmed Business Owners Actually Talk About Being Trapped" and "What Makes the Reluctant MD Finally Spend Money").

---

## 1. The book's job

The book is a top-of-funnel authority asset. It is not a sales pitch and it is not a how-to manual. Its job is to name the reader's problem more clearly than anyone ever has, so that the moment of recognition does the work. When the reader feels understood in a way no previous conversation has achieved, the book has succeeded.

It serves two channels at once. It is a direct acquisition asset for readers who find Jake, and it is a tool the introducer network (business coaches, accountants) hands over to pre-sell Jake before an introduction is made. Because the awareness-to-action gap for this reader is 2 to 5 years, the book is a presence strategy, not a closing tool. It plants the diagnosis and waits for the trigger.

---

## 2. The reader: The Reluctant MD

**Persona name:** David Carver.

**Who he is:** Male, early-to-mid 50s. Founder and Managing Director of a small UK B2B service business: professional services, recruitment, training, consulting, or managed services. He built his reputation on deep domain expertise and still sees himself as an expert first and a business owner second.

**The qualifier is a state, not a headcount.** You become the Reluctant MD the moment you have staff and have become the bottleneck. Not a solo operator, not enterprise. The emotional centre is the owner of a small service firm with a team, typically a handful to a few dozen people, who is the constraint on his own business. Avoid pinning the book to a specific employee count.

**His temperament:** A challenger. Straight-talking, high standards, impatient with vague answers, does not suffer fools. Outwardly the competent MD. Inwardly exhausted by how manually the business still runs. He is privately frustrated and will not say most of it out loud.

**What he has already tried, and watched fail:**
- Bought HubSpot or Pipedrive, set it up, watched adoption collapse within six months into a data graveyard.
- Hired a VA or admin assistant, then ended up doing half their job.
- Started documenting processes, got pulled away, left it in a shared folder somewhere.
- Took on a senior hire hoping they would "own" an area. They needed managing too.
- Watched AI demos and webinars and came away more confused about where to start.

He is not looking for another consultant. He is looking for someone who understands both the technology and the business reality, and he has not found them.

## 3. His one problem (and why everyone else gets it wrong)

In his words:

> "I just don't have enough time. I'm in everything. My team can't make a decision without me, my CRM is a mess, and somehow it's still all on me. I didn't build this business to spend my life in it."

The deeper truth: this is not a time-management problem. It is an architecture problem. The business was built around his expertise, not around systems, which makes him not just overworked but structurally irreplaceable. Every decision, client relationship, and process exception flows through him because nothing was ever designed not to. The business cannot grow without him being in it more. That is a design flaw, not a workload issue.

Every previous fix failed for the same reason: it treated the symptom, not the cause. A CRM without a designed process is a database nobody fills in. A VA without automation is more coordination overhead. Documentation without accountability dies in a folder. The missing piece every time was someone who could see the process architecture, redesign it around systems rather than people, and build something a non-technical team would actually use.

**The single most important move in the whole book:** name the bottleneck clearly. It is the thing nobody has articulated for him. The moment it is named, he feels understood. Lead here every time.

---

## 4. The four layers of his problem

**Surface (what he openly complains about):** Not enough time. The team checks everything with him. Costs rising faster than revenue. No day-to-day visibility. The CRM mess. Admin eating the week. Inconsistent onboarding. No confident revenue forecast. Paying for software he barely uses. Good people hard to find and keep.

**Hidden (what he feels but will not say, the 2am thoughts):** "I am the business. If I stepped back, it would collapse." "I preach systemising and delegating to clients, then run my own firm on spreadsheets and good intentions." "I'm embarrassed by how manually we operate." "I've lost confidence I can fix this myself." "I'm scared of who I am if the chaos disappears." "I'm not as in control as I look." "I resent what the business costs me personally." These are the lines the book must name out loud.

**Downstream (the cost of doing nothing):** The business plateaus and cannot grow past him. Burnout becomes inevitable. Key staff leave because nothing gets fixed. A process failure loses a major client. A great referral arrives at the wrong moment and goes elsewhere. Family life deteriorates. Systematised competitors quietly overtake him. He can never take a proper holiday. The business is devalued at exit.

**Aspirational (what he has stopped saying out loud):** A business that runs without him in the room. Two weeks away with his phone off. A team that executes without him. Recurring revenue that does not require winning new clients every month. A business worth selling. Time spent on work that energises him. Pride in how the business operates, not just what it produces. The job is to make these feel specific, reachable, and soon, not vague and far away.

## 5. How he actually talks (use his words, not ours)

The voice-of-customer research is the language bank. The book should sound like it was written from inside his head.

**The metaphors he reaches for, unprompted:**
- **Bottleneck.** The single most common self-diagnosis. He already uses this word. Anchor the book to it.
- **Prison, trap, cage, the business owns me.** "I own a business but it feels like the business owns me." "I'm a prisoner of my own success."
- **Spinning plates.** Constant motion, dread of the one that falls.
- **Firefighting.** Every day reactive, never proactive. "I spend all day putting out fires."
- **Wearing all the hats / wearing too many hats.** Chief Everything Officer.
- **Treadmill, hamster wheel.** Running hard and going nowhere.

**The registers (emotional bands the language sits in):**
- **Exhaustion register:** drowning, burning out, passing out, running on empty, "I'm always on", 7.30 to 9pm, no free time, evenings and holidays tethered, phone never off.
- **Identity register:** "I am the business", "the business is me", Chief Everything Officer, indispensable, irreplaceable. His worth and his overwork are tangled together.
- **Failed-fix register:** "tried everything", "glorified spreadsheet", "it didn't stick", money wasted on tools and half-finished projects, a CRM that became a graveyard, a VA who needed managing.
- **Aspiration register:** "runs without me", "back to the work I love", time off with the phone off, a business that is an asset not a cage, "doing what only I can do".

**How he searches (the questions he types):**
- Symptom-level: "How do I stop doing everything myself?", "Small business owner overwhelmed", "How do I get my team to use the CRM?"
- Diagnosis-level: "How do I stop being the bottleneck in my business?", "Why does my business depend so much on me?", "How to create a self-managing business".
- Solution-level: "How to run my business without me?", "How to build a business I can sell", "How to scale a consulting business beyond just one person".

## 6. What finally makes him buy

He will sit in pain for years. The book and the brand have to be present when the trigger arrives, because the decision is emotional first and justified with numbers afterward.

**The emotional drivers, in sequence:** Fear breaks the procrastination. Aspiration sustains the commitment through the work. They are sequential, not competing. Loss aversion runs at roughly twice the strength of gain motivation for this reader, so the content names the cost of the status quo rather than manufacturing hype.

**The strongest single trigger:** the moment business cost becomes relationship cost. When the business consumes something that matters to someone who matters to him: a missed holiday, an absent presence at home, a partner who says "you who never have time". That is when "interesting" becomes "now".

**Other practical triggers and deadlines:** a failed-implementation low (the CRM finally abandoned), a family pressure point, a revenue plateau that no extra hours can move, a key-person scare (someone vital nearly leaves or does), a health warning, and calendar moments: January reset, post-summer return in September, year-end planning, and the ongoing 2025 to 2026 UK cost pressure (rising employment costs and tighter margins making manual operating models unaffordable).

**How he justifies it once he has emotionally decided:** ROI, time recovered, the cost of his own hours, capacity to grow, and a business that becomes a sellable asset. Lead the emotion, then arm him with the numbers to defend the decision to himself, his co-director, or his accountant.

**The trust mechanism:** the trusted referral is the primary route in. Around 84% of B2B decisions start with a referral. He buys people he trusts, validated by people he already trusts. This is why the introducer network and the challenger-honest voice matter more than any funnel mechanic.

## 7. How he defines success

Two versions sit underneath the same words, and the book should speak to both:
- **The freedom dream:** time, holidays, phone off, life back, escape from the cage.
- **The control dream:** a business that is a real asset, predictable, sellable, professional, something he is proud to show under the hood.

He wants the inside of the business to finally match how it looks from the outside. "Back to the work I love" recurs: he does not want to stop working, he wants to do only the work that requires his actual brain and hand the rest to systems.

## 8. Jake's positioning and voice (how the book should feel)

**Positioning:** the "Who Not How" partner. The reader already knows what he should do strategically. The gap is technical implementation. Jake is not a strategic validator, he is the person who builds the thing and makes it stick.

**Why Jake, specifically:** 30 years in technology change, half in enterprise (banks, global transformation), half in SME. He brings enterprise-grade systems architecture to a 15-person firm without the enterprise price tag or the consultant's incentive to keep billing. He runs the same kind of business he is selling: The Automatic Business is itself built on automation, recurring revenue, and systemised delivery. The reader is buying a proof of concept, not a theory.

**Credibility anchors to use:** named case studies with real numbers, not vague testimonials. Matt's 19-step recruitment process. Laura's 600 emails per quarter. Before and after, with measurable outcomes.

**Contrarian positions Jake holds publicly:**
- Most "we need AI" enquiries resolve into basic automation that has existed for a decade. Name the AI FOMO honestly.
- "Your staff shortage is a process failure." The instinct to hire or manage harder is usually the wrong fix.
- It was never the tool, it was the process design. This reframes the burned CRM from a buying objection into a buying reason.

**Voice:** challenger, honest, sharp, willing to be uncomfortable. Jake does not tell the reader what he wants to hear, and that is exactly what makes him trusted. Prefer content that is sharp and a little uncomfortable over content that sounds polished but safe.

**Proprietary IP to weave in:** the HAI Triage Framework (Human / Automation / AI ownership of every task), Process Archaeology (the deep diagnostic), the Missions Catalogue (modular pre-built automation bundles), the Matt Test (every system must be operable by a non-technical person in a single action), and the "Only Do What Only You Can Do" principle. Tagline: **"Do What Only You Can Do."**

---

## 9. Strategic spine for the book

Lead with the bottleneck diagnosis. It opens every door. Then sequence the emotional logic: name the hidden 2am fears to create recognition, make the downstream cost concrete to create urgency, then make the aspirational state feel specific and near to sustain commitment. Use his exact language and metaphors throughout. Arm the reader with the numbers to justify the decision after the emotion has landed. Keep Jake's voice challenger-honest, not salesy.

The commercial logic the book quietly supports: admin elimination is the quick win, CRM overhaul is the structural fix, and the full bottleneck engagement is the premium offer. The book never sells these directly. It earns the right to the conversation by naming the problem.

## 10. House style and formatting rules

- **Never use em-dashes.** Replace with a full stop and a new sentence (for punch), a comma (as a direct swap), or a colon (when the following text defines the preceding term).
- Write in his register. Plain, direct, British. No consultant jargon unless he would use it.
- Use named, numbered case studies over abstract claims.
- Name the cost of the status quo rather than manufacturing urgency.
- Sharp and slightly uncomfortable beats polished and safe.
